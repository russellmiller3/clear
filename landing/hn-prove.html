1. ▲ Show HN: A `rule` keyword that proves your business logic and shows your CRO PROVED next to each policy (buildclear.dev)
712 points by rmiller 4 hours ago | flag | hide | 258 comments
Author here. Most B2B apps I've shipped have one or two business policies that matter more than anything else. "Discounts over 30% need VP approval." "Refunds over $500 need a CFO sign-off." "Big enterprise deals need the CRO." When something goes wrong, that's the rule a board member asks about.

Today these rules are buried in middleware, sprinkled across endpoints, half-tested. Audit time you write a slide deck explaining what was supposed to happen.

Clear has a rule keyword that names the policy and proves it:
rule discount-cap-thirty:
  enforce that deal's discount_percent is less than 30,
    or fail with error message: 'Discounts of 30% or more require VP approval'

rule big-deals-need-cro:
  if deal's amount is greater than 100000:
    enforce that deal's approver_role is 'cro',
      or fail with error message: 'deals over $100k need the CRO'
Run clear prove deal-desk.clear:
Business rules in this file:
  [PROVED]       discount-cap-thirty (line 1) — for every possible deal
  [PROVED]       big-deals-need-cro (line 5) — for every possible deal
  [UNVERIFIABLE] customer-must-exist (line 11) — body calls the database

  2 of 3 rules proved. 1 unverifiable.
The CRO sees PROVED next to "discount-cap-thirty" and trusts it. Not because they read the code — because the verdict is attributed by name and the prover is a small, auditable program.

UNVERIFIABLE is the honest one. The rule looks up data from a database; the math engine can't enumerate every possible DB state, so it refuses to claim universal correctness. Runtime check still fires. We tell customers exactly which rules are math-proved and which are runtime-only.

Demo + sample audit PDF: buildclear.dev/prove.

aphyr 4 hours ago | parent | prev | next [–]
What's the math engine? Bounded model checker, SMT, abstract interpretation, custom?
rmiller 4 hours ago | root | parent | next [–]
Custom symbolic simplifier — small, single-file, walks the AST and folds expressions over the rule's free variables. Handles arithmetic equality and inequality, boolean composition, and bounded conditionals. We deliberately picked a tiny verifiable engine over an SMT solver because the engine ITSELF needs to be auditable — if a CRO asks "how do I trust the prover?" the answer should be "here's the file, it's 800 lines of JS."

The cost is expressive limits. Rules with non-linear arithmetic, unbounded loops, or floats outside narrow ranges hit UNVERIFIABLE. We treat that as a feature: the prover is honest about its scope. Hooking in Z3 as an option for power users is a future cycle.

"Total by default" is the part of this that interests me most. Most languages let you have side effects anywhere; Clear flips the default and makes you write live: when you want effects. That's the same instinct as Haskell's IO monad but expressed in plain English instead of monadic plumbing.

Does the fence actually matter, or is it ceremonial?
rmiller 3 hours ago | root | parent | next [–]
It does real work. The prover walks rule bodies and refuses to give a PROVED verdict to anything that contains a node it classifies as effectful — DB lookups, HTTP requests, AI calls, time reads. The live: block is what marks those nodes. So when a developer writes a rule that should be pure and accidentally calls the database, the prover catches it: UNVERIFIABLE with a reason ("body calls the database").

The customer-facing version: every PROVED rule is mathematically guaranteed regardless of database state. Every UNVERIFIABLE rule has at least one effect inside its body. The fence is what makes the verdict trustworthy.
simonw 3 hours ago | parent | prev | next [–]
Quick sanity check on the runtime witness piece — is this just "you wrote a test that asserts 400 on a violation"? Or something stronger?
rmiller 3 hours ago | root | parent | next [–]
Stronger. The harness:
1. Compiles your .clear file
2. Spawns the compiled Express server on a free port
3. Generates 20 random inputs that VIOLATE each rule (using the rule's symbolic constraints to pick adversarial values)
4. Fires them at the live server
5. Asserts 100% come back as 4xx with the rule's name in the response body

If even one violator slips through with success, the prover lied. We've caught compiler bugs that way — symbolic proof said PROVED but the runtime guard had a bug. The witness is the canary.

A real PDF generated by clear audit deal-desk.clear. Plus a session header with the file's commit SHA, the prover version, and the date.

Compliance buyers want a snapshot they can hand to their auditor. The PDF is that snapshot. Every customer's deploy generates one.

What happens when a rule is mathematically PROVED but has a bug in its specification? The prover is happy, the CRO is happy, but the rule is doing the wrong thing.
rmiller 2 hours ago | root | parent | next [–]
That's the right question. The prover proves "this rule rejects every input that violates the condition you wrote." It does NOT prove "the condition you wrote is the right condition." Those are different things and we're explicit about it.

The mitigation is in the workflow: rules read in plain English, so a non-engineer can review them. discount-cap-thirty says "discount must be less than 30" — the CRO reads it directly, no translation. That's the bug-in-spec defense, and it works because the spec IS readable by the person who owns the policy.

There's no good answer to "did you write the right rule?" in any system. The answer to "did you implement the rule you wrote correctly?" is what the prover gives you.
