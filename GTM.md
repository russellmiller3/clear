## Top Competitor

**Retool is the top competitor.**

Not Kore.

Not UiPath.

Not Relay.

Retool is the closest money competitor because it already owns the phrase "build internal tools fast."

### Retool's Strength

Retool is mature.

It has:

- Apps.
- Workflows.
- Agents.
- Permissions.
- Audit logs.
- Source control.
- Enterprise hosting.
- Big logos.

Their official pricing page says developers are the common builders. It also prices by builder users, internal users, workflow runs, and agent hours.

Source: https://retool.com/pricing

### Turn The Strength Into The Weakness

Retool's strength is that it is a platform.

That is also the weakness.

Platforms create ownership questions:

- Who learns it?
- Who builds in it?
- Who maintains it?
- Who fixes broken workflows?
- Who pays for builder seats?
- Who tracks workflow runs?
- Who owns the agent budget?

Clear's counter-frame:

> Retool gives you a platform your team has to operate. Clear gives you the finished workflow, operated for you.

### The Retool Attack Line

Use this in calls:

> Retool is great if you have a developer ready to own internal tools. Clear is for when ops needs the workflow gone and engineering is not coming.

Do not say Retool is bad.

Say Retool is too much ownership for the buyer's actual problem.

Why:

## Qualification Rules

Only pursue a prospect if at least 4 are true:

- The workflow happens weekly or daily.
- Three or more people touch it.
- It currently lives in Slack, email, spreadsheets, or Airtable.
- Delays cost revenue, churn, compliance risk, or executive attention.
- The buyer can approve $5k/month or reach the person who can.
- Engineering has not fixed it after repeated asks.
- The team already tried a tool and still works around it.

If fewer than 4 are true, move on.

This is a pain filter.

Use it brutally.

## Discovery Call Script

### Opening

> I am not going to pitch for the first half.
>
> I want to understand one workflow your team hates.
>
> If it is painful enough, I will tell you what I would build.
>
> If it is not, I will say that too.

### Questions

Ask in this order:

1. What workflow do people complain about every week?
2. Where does it live today?
3. Who touches it?
4. What happens when it is late or wrong?
5. What systems does it need to read or update?
6. Who approves exceptions?
7. How often does the process change?
8. What has the team tried already?
9. Why has engineering not fixed it?
10. If this vanished next week, what would that be worth?

### Listen For

Strong signals:

- "Slack."
- "Spreadsheet."
- "Manual approval."
- "People forget."
- "Sales is mad."
- "CS is chasing."
- "Nobody trusts the data."
- "Engineering has no bandwidth."

Weak signals:

- "It would be nice."
- "Maybe someday."
- "We are evaluating platforms."
- "Procurement needs to review."
- "Can you send a deck?"

Strong signals get a close.

Weak signals get a polite exit.

## Objections

### "Why not Retool?"

> Retool is good when you want your team to build and own internal tools.
>
> Clear is better when the business wants the workflow gone without creating another platform owner.

### "Can we try it free?"

> No free pilots.
>
> If the pain is real, the audit is $1,500 or the build starts at $5k.
>
> If I cannot make the first version usable, I refund the setup.

### "We need SOC 2."

> Then this is probably not the first workflow.
>
> Start with a lower-risk internal process or come back when the compliance path is ready.

### "Can our team edit it?"

> Yes, the source is readable.
>
> But the main value is that you do not have to own the maintenance.

### "Is this AI?"

> It uses AI where AI helps.
>
> The product is the workflow app, not an AI demo.
